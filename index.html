Temperature Shift

Run cold water over your wrists or splash your face. This activates your body's dive reflex — it immediately slows your heart rate and lowers arousal. Fast, simple, no equipment needed.

⚠️ If you have heart, blood pressure, or circulation concerns, check with your care team first.

Cold water on the face triggers the diving reflex — an automatic physiological response that slows your heart rate and shifts blood flow within seconds. It doesn't require thought or effort. It's one of the few techniques that works on the nervous system directly rather than through the mind. Even cold water on your wrists activates a related pathway through sudden sensory contrast.

Or try something else:

Physiological Sigh

Double inhale through the nose (breathe in, then sniff a little more), then one long slow exhale through your mouth. Repeat 2–3 times. Research shows even one round noticeably lowers stress.

When stressed, your lungs don't fully expand. The double inhale fills them completely. The long exhale tells your nervous system it's safe to relax.

Paired Muscle Relaxation

Tense a muscle group as you inhale — try your fists, shoulders, or jaw. Hold for 5 seconds. Release completely as you exhale. Notice the contrast. Repeat 2–3 times.

Under stress, your muscles hold low-level tension without your awareness. Deliberately tensing a muscle group first makes that tension conscious and then gives it somewhere to go. The contrast between tension and release trains your nervous system to recognize what relaxation actually feels like — which becomes increasingly hard to detect under chronic stress. A few rounds also engages focused attention, which competes with anxious thought loops.

Feet on the Floor

Press your feet firmly into the ground. Move your toes. Say quietly: "I am in [room]. I am okay right now." Repeat until you feel more anchored.

Feeling the weight of your body sends a direct signal to your nervous system that you are here and safe. Especially helpful when you feel checked out or disconnected.

Working with heavy feelings

Name It to Tame It

Labelling an emotion — "I'm angry," "I feel grief" — actually reduces its intensity in the brain. The more specific, the better. "I feel embarrassed" calms more than "I feel bad."

Where do you feel it in your body? Tight chest → anxiety or anger. Heavy chest → grief. Heat in face → anger or shame. Stomach drop → fear.

Brain imaging research shows that putting a word to a feeling activates the prefrontal cortex — the thinking brain — while reducing activity in the amygdala, which drives emotional reactivity. This happens even with a single word. The more specific the label, the stronger the effect — "I feel embarrassed" produces more settling than "I feel bad." You're not dismissing the feeling. You're changing its signal strength.

Guilt vs Shame — An Important Difference

Guilt says: "I did something I don't feel good about." It's about behaviour — and can guide repair.
Shame says: "I am bad, broken, unworthy." It's about identity — and shuts people down.

Which one are you carrying right now?

Guilt can motivate repair and change. Shame tends to create hiding, avoidance, and escape — and increases risk. Self-compassion makes accountability feel safe enough to stay with.

Emotions Come in Waves

Even strong feelings usually peak and pass within minutes when not fought or avoided. Staying present without escaping — that's how emotions move through instead of getting stuck.

"I can stay with this feeling. It will rise and fall."

Emotions are temporary — they have a beginning, a peak, and an end. What stretches them out is what happens when they arrive. Fighting an emotion — trying to suppress it, reason it away, or escape it — keeps the emotional loop active. Avoiding it delays its completion. When you stay present with a feeling without trying to change it, you allow it to move through its natural arc. The peak itself is usually minutes. It's the story we tell afterward — and the effort to push it away — that makes it feel permanent.

What's Underneath the Anger

Anger often protects something more vulnerable. Ask: "If I weren't angry right now, what would I be feeling?" Hurt, fear, and shame are common answers. That's usually where the real need lives.

Anger can be a primary emotion — but often it's secondary, covering something that feels more exposing: hurt, fear, grief, shame. Acting from the surface emotion tends to miss the actual need. Asking "what would I feel if I weren't angry?" creates a moment of honest contact with what's actually driving it. That's where the real work — and the real relief — usually lives.

You don't have to resolve the anger. Just get one layer closer to what's underneath it.

Too much → Finding direction

Feeling stuck is information, not failure.

Finding Direction

Control Check

Ask: "Is this something I can actually influence right now?"

  • Yes → Take one small action. Just one.
  • No → Move to acceptance and grounding. Rumination pretends to be problem-solving. It usually isn't.
Research shows rumination increases anxiety while reducing your ability to solve things. The control check interrupts this by asking one question: can I act on this or not? If not, acceptance tools — not more thinking.

One Small Thing

What's the single smallest action you could take today? Not the solution — just the next right step. What's the easiest, most manageable first move?

Action doesn't have to be big to be meaningful. One small thing is still forward motion.

When you're stuck, the brain narrows — options shrink, problems feel monolithic, and anything short of a complete solution feels pointless. This is why large goals fail under distress and small actions don't. A small action doesn't require the problem to be solved first. It just requires one step to be possible right now. Small actions also produce evidence: proof that movement is possible, which directly counters the paralysis that stress creates. Momentum is built from small, repeated steps — not from waiting until you feel ready.

Sort What's Yours to Carry

Some of what's weighing on you might not actually be yours. Which of your current challenges feel personal — rooted in your habits or thoughts? Which are shaped by external things — work demands, housing, systems, or other people's choices?

Just because someone handed it to you doesn't mean it was yours to hold.

People under chronic stress often carry a mix of personal challenges and systemic ones — poverty, discrimination, housing instability, workplace inequity — but experience all of it as personal failure. Sorting what is genuinely yours from what belongs to a system or another person isn't denial — it's accuracy. When you stop fighting something that isn't yours, you free up real energy for the parts that are. The load doesn't disappear, but it becomes the right size.

If/Then Plan

When you don't know what to do next, commit to one conditional:

"If [this moment], I will [one small action]."

Example: "If I'm still stuck after 10 minutes, I'll text one person."

You're not solving it. You're giving yourself a direction when the moment comes.

If/then plans — implementation intentions — are one of the most reliably effective behaviour change tools in research. Deciding in advance what you'll do in a specific situation removes the need for willpower in the moment. The decision is already made. You just follow through on a plan your calmer self created.

Too much → Connection

Loneliness in hard times is real, and often the least talked about part.

Connection + Support

Reach Out — One Person, Right Now

Not a grand gesture. One text or call to someone safe. "Hey, thinking of you" counts. Connection doesn't have to be a conversation about your struggles to matter.

Having even one supportive person measurably reduces distress. Your brain is wired to share its load with others — problems feel smaller and more manageable when someone else is present.

Hyperindependence

"I can only rely on myself" is often a survival strategy, not a preference. If asking for help feels dangerous or shameful, that's worth exploring. Isolation increases risk. Connection protects.

Hyperindependence usually develops in environments where depending on others was genuinely unsafe — where help didn't come, where vulnerability was exploited, or where self-sufficiency was the only reliable option. It's a learned protection, not a personality trait. In recovery, it can feel like strength — but isolation is consistently one of the strongest predictors of relapse and worsening mental health. Recognizing "I don't need anyone" as a survival response, not a preference, creates the possibility of slowly and safely testing whether that's still true.

Tools to break the loop

Control Check

Ask: "Is this something I can actually influence right now?" If yes — take one small action. If no — move to grounding. Rumination pretends to be problem-solving. It usually isn't.

Rumination activates the same brain regions as genuine problem-solving, which is why it feels like you're doing something useful. Research consistently shows the opposite — rumination increases depression and anxiety while actually impairing your ability to solve problems. The control check interrupts this by forcing a single binary question: can I act on this right now, or not? If yes, one small action breaks the loop. If no, you've just removed the justification for continuing to circle it.

Leaves on a Stream

Imagine a gently flowing stream. Each thought that shows up — place it on a leaf and watch it drift away without chasing it. You're the observer, not the thought.

Instead of getting caught up in your thoughts, you practise watching them from a distance. You can't stop your thoughts — and fighting them takes energy. This is easier: notice them, place them on a leaf, let them pass.

Worry Postponement

Write down everything on your mind. Tell yourself: "I'll come back to this at [specific time]." The thoughts don't need to be solved right now. Give your brain permission to set them down.

Most people find that when they return to the worry later, it doesn't feel as urgent or scary.

Trying to stop a worry usually makes it worse — the brain interprets the suppression effort as confirmation that the thought is dangerous, and keeps circling back to it. Postponement works differently: instead of fighting the thought, you acknowledge it and give it a specific time. This sends a signal that the thought has been received and doesn't need to keep repeating. Writing it down adds another layer — externalising the worry onto paper reduces the brain's need to hold it internally. Most people find the urgency drops considerably when they return.

Thinking → Self-talk

The way you talk to yourself matters — especially when things are hard.

Managing Self-Talk

"I'm Having the Thought That…"

Instead of "I'm a failure," try "I'm having the thought that I'm a failure." This creates space between you and the thought — reducing its authority without dismissing it.

Adding "I'm having the thought that..." makes the thought something you're observing — a mental event, not reality. Research shows this reduces emotional intensity without needing to argue with the thought.

The Friend Test

Take the thought. Would you say that exact thing to a close friend who was struggling? If not — it's not fair to say it to yourself either. What would the kinder, more accurate version be?

Most people apply significantly harsher standards to themselves than to anyone they care about — but the double standard is usually not conscious. There's often a genuine belief that "my situation is different" or "I deserve the criticism." The friend test creates a moment of honest comparison that's hard to dismiss. It doesn't tell you what to think — it just highlights the inconsistency, which is often enough to loosen the self-critical loop.

Thought Diffusion — Lighten the Grip

Try one of these to loosen a sticky thought:

  • Say it out loud in a cartoon voice
  • Sing it to the tune of "Happy Birthday"
  • Write it down and read it slowly — like a script, not a verdict

You don't have to delete the thought. Just loosen its grip.

Thoughts feel authoritative partly because of how seriously we hold them. Singing a thought to a cartoon tune or saying it in a silly voice doesn't change its content — it changes its relationship to you. Instead of the thought feeling like a fact about reality, it becomes something your mind produced. This technique comes from ACT — cognitive defusion — and research shows it reduces the emotional impact and believability of thoughts without needing to argue with them or replace them with something positive.

Thinking → Patterns

Thinking patterns aren't character flaws — they're mind habits. The brain filling in gaps too confidently.

Common Thinking Traps

11 Patterns Worth Knowing

  • Catastrophizing — jumping to worst-case
  • All-or-nothing — no middle ground
  • Mind reading — assuming what others think
  • Fortune telling — predicting failure
  • Emotional reasoning — "I feel like a failure, so I must be"
  • Should statements — rigid rules that create shame
  • Labelling — "I am a failure" vs "I failed at this"
  • Personalization — taking blame for things outside your control
  • Filtering — noticing only the negative
  • Overgeneralising — one event becomes a permanent pattern
  • Magnifying/minimising — inflating the bad, shrinking the good

Which ones feel familiar? You don't have to change them — just naming the pattern interrupts the automatic assumption that the thought is simply true.

Cognitive distortions are systematic — they don't skew randomly, they reliably skew toward threat, failure, or worthlessness. That's why they feel so convincing. When you name the pattern — "that's catastrophizing" — you interrupt the automatic acceptance that what you're thinking is simply true. The thought doesn't disappear, but it loses its authority. That gap between "thought" and "fact" is where change becomes possible.

Thought Record — 4 Steps

  1. Write the thought exactly as it came
  2. Name the pattern (from the list above)
  3. Rewrite it in a fair, honest way — as you'd say to a friend
  4. Notice: did the feeling shift even slightly?
The thought record doesn't work by replacing negative thoughts with positive ones — your brain rejects implausible substitutions. It works by building cognitive flexibility: the ability to generate more than one interpretation of the same event. Under stress, the brain fixes on one reading and treats it as fact. The thought record practises generating alternatives. Even a small shift in certainty — from "this is definitely true" to "this might be true" — changes how much power the thought has.

Thinking → Wise Mind

Wise Mind is where your feelings and your thinking work together — the calm answer, not the loud one.

Accessing Wise Mind

Three Mind States

Emotion Mind — feelings drive decisions. Fast, reactive, intense.
Rational Mind — logic leads, disconnected from what matters.
Wise Mind — the balance point. You notice feelings and think clearly. It often feels like: "This is the next right step."

Most people assume that the more rational they are, the better their decisions. Research says otherwise. Emotion Mind alone is reactive and short-sighted. Reasonable Mind alone strips out the values and relationships that make a decision actually right for you. Wise Mind integrates both — you feel what you feel and you also think clearly. The goal isn't to eliminate emotion from decision-making. It's to stop either mind from running the show alone.

How to Access It Right Now

  • Put both feet flat on the floor
  • Take one slow breath in and out — feel your chest move
  • Ask: "What feels true right now?"
  • Listen for the calm answer, not the loud one

Wise Mind tends to be calm and certain. Emotion Mind is urgent. The quiet, steady answer is usually Wise Mind.

You can't access Wise Mind when your body is flooded — the physical grounding steps come first for a reason. Feet on the floor, one slow breath, feeling your chest move — each of these shifts your body's stress response enough to let the thinking brain re-engage. The question "what feels true right now?" accesses a quieter kind of knowing than direct logical analysis. Wise Mind tends to speak at the same volume as everything else — it's just not urgent. The grounding creates the conditions to hear it.

Understand myself → Values

Values don't disappear when things get hard. They point forward even when everything else feels unclear.

The Values Vault

What Are Values?

Values are directions, not destinations. You don't complete them — you live toward them. They're not what you should want. They're what actually matters to you, in your bones.

Examples: honesty, connection, freedom, stability, creativity, family, growth, contribution, health, justice, belonging.

Goals can be missed, abandoned, or completed. Values don't go away. That's why they're more reliable as a compass — especially when motivation drops, when circumstances change, or when external pressure makes it hard to know what you actually want. Acting in the direction of a genuine value produces more sustained engagement than acting out of fear or obligation. The key word is genuine — values that come from "what I should care about" don't hold the same weight as ones that come from "what matters to me in my bones."

Finding Yours — Three Questions

  1. When have you felt most alive or most yourself?
  2. What makes you angry about the world? (Often a clue to what you care about.)
  3. If you could only keep three things — relationships, work, freedoms, aspects of yourself — what would they be?
Anger points at violation — something that matters to you is being threatened, ignored, or blocked. That's why it's a reliable clue to values. If injustice makes you furious, justice matters to you. If people being dismissed makes you angry, dignity matters to you. This doesn't mean acting on the anger — it means using it as information about what's important. The same logic applies to grief: what you grieve losing tells you what you valued having.

Values in Action

Choose one value. Ask: "What's the smallest action I could take today that moves me toward this?"

You're not deciding forever — just the next right direction.

When you're overwhelmed, a list of values is just more things to weigh. One value functions as a compass — it reduces the decision to a single question: does this move me toward or away from what matters? Research on values-based action shows that even a brief connection to a genuine value before making a decision improves follow-through and reduces distress. The key word is genuine: a value borrowed from what you think you should care about doesn't carry the same weight as one you actually feel.

Understand myself → Inner voices

That critical voice in your head isn't the whole story. It's one narrator — with a particular perspective, shaped by specific experiences.

Who's Talking in There?

Name the Voice

Your stress or inner critic isn't you — it's a character passing through. Give it a name: "The Inner Critic," "The Alarm Bell," "The Fence-Sitter," "The Backseat Driver from Hell."

What role does it play? What would you name it?

By externalizing the voice, you create distance between you and the problem — making it easier to manage. The voice becomes something you can observe and question, rather than something you are.

Question It

Once you've named the voice, ask:

  • Where did it learn to talk like that?
  • What is it trying to protect you from?
  • What would a more empowering narrator say instead?
Inner critics don't appear from nowhere — they're usually learned from specific environments, relationships, or experiences where that voice served a protective function. Asking "where did this learn to talk like that?" shifts it from being a fact about you to being a response that developed in a context. That's not the same as dismissing it. Often the inner critic was genuinely trying to protect you — from rejection, failure, or danger. Understanding its origin makes it easier to work with rather than against.

Narrator Swap

Imagine the same thought spoken by a wise friend, a calm therapist, or an older version of yourself who has been through hard things. How would they say it? What would they add?

These alternative narratives aren't less true — they're often more accurate, because they include context and compassion the critic leaves out.

The inner critic presents itself as the objective voice — but it's one narrator with a particular perspective, shaped by specific experiences. It tends to omit context, minimize strengths, and overweight failures. A wise friend or your older self isn't being falsely positive — they're adding information the critic systematically leaves out. Alternative narratives work not because they're more comforting but because they're more complete. That's what makes them clinically meaningful, not just emotionally soothing.

Understand myself → Control

Not everything that lands on your plate is yours to solve.

Spot What You Can Control

Signs You CAN Influence This

  • You can personally take action that would change or improve it
  • Your thoughts, emotions, or behaviours directly impact the outcome
  • If you change your approach, the situation might improve

Signs This Is OUTSIDE Your Control

  • No matter what you do, the outcome stays the same
  • Other people's decisions, policies, or systems shape the outcome
  • You've tried multiple strategies, and nothing changes

If it's outside your control — accepting this isn't giving up. It's putting your energy where it can actually do something.

Giving up means withdrawing care. Acceptance means stopping the fight against what can't be changed — so you have energy left for what can. Research consistently shows that accepting uncontrollable stressors lowers distress more effectively than continued attempts to change them. This is especially relevant when the stressor is systemic — housing insecurity, discrimination, chronic illness. You're not surrendering to it. You're refusing to let the battle against an unmovable wall exhaust everything you have.

What Can You Actually Do Today?

If you have influence: what's one small, concrete action you could take?
If you don't: what grounding or acceptance tool can help you carry this without fighting it?

Under high stress, the brain narrows its focus — options shrink, the problem feels monolithic, and any action that isn't a complete solution feels pointless. That's why big goals fail under distress and small actions don't. A small action doesn't need the problem to be solved first. It just needs to be doable right now. Over time, small actions compound. They also produce evidence that movement is possible, which counters the paralysis that chronic stress creates.

Understand myself → Identity

Recovery and growth are not about going back to who you were. They're about choosing who you are becoming.

Identity & Reconstruction

Survival Identities vs Chosen Identities

Some identities formed to help you survive — "the strong one who doesn't need anything," "the reliable one who never asks for help." These were proof you became who you needed to be.

Recovery gives you room to choose differently. Not erasing who you were — adding back what got left out.

Survival identities aren't weaknesses — they're adaptive responses to real environments. "The strong one who doesn't need anything" made sense in a situation where showing need was unsafe or ineffective. "The one who holds it together" worked when someone had to. The problem is that these roles are context-specific, but they tend to generalize. In recovery and growth, the old environment may no longer be present — but the identity persists because it worked, and the brain doesn't easily let go of what worked. Recognizing it as a response rather than a fixed truth is the first step to expanding it.

Reconstruction Prompts

  • "One part of me I want to reconnect with is ___."
  • "One part of me that survived despite everything is ___."
  • "The person I'm building toward includes ___."

You are not starting over. You are adding to your story with more intention.

Affirmations — "I am strong, I am worthy" — often fail because they make a claim the brain doesn't yet believe. These prompts work differently. They don't ask you to declare something. They ask you to notice and name specific things that are already true: a part of you that survived, a part you want to reconnect with. That's not a statement about who you are — it's evidence. Evidence accumulates in a way that declarations don't. The brain updates on concrete specifics much more readily than on general positive claims.

Not everything hard in your life is a personal failing. Some of it is the system.

It's Not Just You

Seeing the Bigger Picture

Stress doesn't only come from inside us. It also comes from housing insecurity, racism, poverty, ableism, workplace inequity, lack of access to services, and systems that weren't built for everyone.

When you internalize a system's failure as a personal flaw, you carry a weight that was never yours.

Refusing to internalize a system's failure is an act of resistance. Naming the systemic context of your stress doesn't excuse you from the work of change — it gives you accurate information about what's actually going on, so you can direct your energy wisely.

Sort What's Personal vs Systemic

Which of your current challenges feel rooted in your habits, thoughts, or relationships? Which are shaped by external things — work demands, housing, policies, discrimination, access?

Are there areas where both overlap?

When people internalize systemic problems as personal failures, they direct their energy at changing themselves instead of addressing what's actually driving the distress. This is especially common among people experiencing poverty, racism, housing instability, or chronic health conditions — stressors that are real and structural, not personal deficiencies. Making the distinction doesn't remove responsibility where it genuinely exists. It ensures the work you do is aimed at the right target.

When the System Is the Problem — Your Role Looks Different

What boundary needs to be set? Who can support you? What action protects your values and your wellbeing without requiring you to fix something that isn't yours to fix?

In clinical practice, helping someone "cope better" with a genuinely unjust or harmful situation can inadvertently reinforce the idea that the problem is theirs to manage. When the stressor is structural — discrimination, poverty, a dehumanizing system — the right clinical move is sometimes naming that the system has failed, setting a boundary against internalising it, and identifying what action protects the person's values and wellbeing. Refusing to carry what isn't yours is an act of self-respect, not passivity.

Emotions → Going deeper

Emotions are signals, not problems. Let's look at what yours might be saying.

What's the Emotion Underneath?

What you feel on the surface is often a secondary emotion — anger, irritability, numbness. Underneath is usually a primary feeling: hurt, fear, shame, grief.

Ask: "If I weren't angry right now, what would I be feeling?" That's usually where the real need lives.

Emotion → Need → Expression

Every emotion points to a need. Try:

  • "I feel ___ → I need ___ → I can express it by ___."
  • Example: "I feel lonely → I need connection → I can reach out to one person."

Naming the need — not just the feeling — is one of the most powerful skills you can build.

Most emotional communication stops at the feeling — "I'm angry," "I'm hurt" — which leaves others with no clear path to respond. Naming the underlying need gives the other person something they can actually act on. It also changes the communication from an accusation to information. When you say "I need support" instead of "you never help," you're harder to argue with and easier to respond to. In recovery and mental health, many people have spent years suppressing or hiding needs entirely — learning to name them directly is a genuine skill that takes practice.

Rewrite the Myth

Which of these feel familiar?

  • "There's a right way to feel in every situation." → Emotions are responses, not grades.
  • "Being emotional means being out of control." → Feeling deeply and responding wisely are not opposites.
  • "Painful emotions should be ignored." → Ignored emotions don't disappear. They build pressure.

The Thought Distortion Decoder

Pick the pattern that sounds most like what showed up for you:

All-or-nothing thinking

"If I mess this up, I've failed completely."
Trying to: Avoid gray areas. Try instead: "It's allowed to be imperfect and still count."

Catastrophizing

"This is going to ruin everything."
Trying to: Prepare for disaster. Try instead: "Hard? Yes. End of the world? Not quite."

Should statements

"I should be over this by now."
Trying to: Maintain control. Try instead: "It's taking the time it takes."

Labelling

"I'm just a failure."
Trying to: Simplify pain. Try instead: "I'm struggling with something. That doesn't define me."

Mind reading

"They think I'm annoying."
Trying to: Avoid rejection by predicting it. Try instead: "I can't know that — unless I ask."

Emotional reasoning

"I feel like a failure, so I must be."
Trying to: Trust internal signals. Try instead: "Feeling it doesn't make it true."

Personalization

"This is my fault."
Trying to: Maintain a sense of control. Try instead: "What actually caused this — and how much of it was mine?"

Filtering

"Everything went wrong."
Trying to: Protect against future disappointment. Try instead: "What actually went wrong — and what didn't?"

Fortune telling

"I know it's going to go badly."
Trying to: Prepare for failure before it happens. Try instead: "That's a prediction, not a fact. What's the actual evidence?"

Overgeneralising

"Nothing ever works out for me."
Trying to: Make sense of repeated pain. Try instead: "This happened. That doesn't mean it always happens."

Magnifying / minimising

"That mistake ruined everything" / "The good parts don't really count."
Trying to: Protect against being caught off guard. Try instead: "What's the actual size of this, honestly?"

Ride It Out

This feeling rises, peaks, and falls on its own — usually within 10–20 minutes if you don't act on it. You don't have to make it disappear. You just have to stay with it a little longer.

  • Name what you feel pulled toward — just to yourself, not out loud
  • Notice where it sits in your body — heat, tension, restlessness
  • Breathe out slowly. Keep noticing. Don't engage with it.
Every time you stay with a strong urge without acting on it, you're demonstrating to yourself that it's survivable. The urge itself doesn't get weaker immediately — but your relationship to it changes. You're no longer someone who acts automatically. You're someone who can wait. That's not small.

Or try something else:

Check the Basics First

Strong urges often attach to unmet needs. Before anything else: are you hungry, angry, lonely, or tired? Address the underlying need and the pull often softens.

Urges don't appear from nowhere — they attach to states the body is already in. Hunger, exhaustion, isolation, and accumulated stress all lower your threshold for acting on impulse. Addressing the actual unmet need is often more effective than trying to fight the urge directly.

Buy Yourself 10 Minutes

You don't have to decide never. You just have to wait 10 minutes. Do something physical — walk, drink cold water, change rooms. Not to fix the feeling. Just to create space between the urge and the action.

If the urge is still there in 10 minutes, you can decide then. Most of the time, the intensity drops.
